# Case Study: BookSwap Campus

BookSwap Campus is a peer-to-peer textbook marketplace designed for university students in Mexico City — specifically those who pay an average MXN 4,800 per semester on required textbooks and the 38% of first-year students who have skipped at least one required book for financial reasons. The platform connects student sellers (carrying books from completed courses) with student buyers (facing the same costs at the start of a new semester) through a verified-registration, ISBN-catalogued, escrow-payment flow at three pilot universities: Universidad Iberoamericana, La Salle, and Panamericana.

This page is the worked-example counterpart to the [agentic-pm-kit playbook](/docs/pm-kit). Every artifact described here was produced by invoking the kit's skills in the order the playbook prescribes — Five Whys ideation, then charter + stakeholder register + product brief, then the full nine-artifact planning suite, then Sprint 4 execution, then a pre-closure projection. Nothing was written by hand outside a skill invocation.

Use this page alongside the playbook chapters — not as a replacement for them. The chapters explain what each skill does and why. This page shows what the output looks like when the kit runs against a real project brief: real names, real dates, real metrics, real open questions. Where the playbook says "the agent will collect …", this page shows what it collected.

## Ideation

See the [Ideation chapter](/docs/pm-kit/02-ideation) of the playbook for what this phase is meant to produce.

The concept memo was produced with the `brainstorming-five-whys` skill against the recurring impediment "fewer than 30% of invited students complete a first transaction within 7 days." The five-layer chain walks from the symptom of onboarding drop-off down to a process-level root cause: the sprint acceptance rubric does not require empirical validation of the user profile before coding critical flows, so onboarding was built around an "ideal" student who rarely exists at the start of a semester. Both corrective actions are concrete and assigned: (1) add a mandatory "empirical user-profile validation" item to the acceptance rubric — owner Project Manager, due before Sprint 5 — and (2) redesign the onboarding flow to separate "verified registration" from "ready to transact" — owner Engineering, due by end of Sprint 4. [See full analysis](/pm-kit/outputs/brainstorming-five-whys/onboarding-friction-sprint-3-4.md)

## Initiation

See the [Initiation chapter](/docs/pm-kit/03-initiation) of the playbook for what this phase is meant to produce.

### Project Charter

The BookSwap Campus v1 charter was produced with the `charter` skill from the concept memo and records the formal authorization of the project by the Office of Educational Innovation at Universidad Iberoamericana Mexico City. Three execution decisions stand out: (1) three SMART objectives with hard dates tied to the academic calendar (go-live 2026-08-15, end-of-semester 2026-12-15), (2) a budget envelope stated as a range (MXN 1.25M–1.75M, ±20%) rather than a false-precision point estimate, and (3) five quantitative success metrics — including "median time-to-first-transaction ≤ 7 days," the exact metric the Five Whys memo flagged as critical. The charter is signature-ready for sponsor, PM, CIO, and the libraries' spokesperson. [See full charter](/pm-kit/outputs/charter/bookswap-campus-v1.md)

### Stakeholder Register

The stakeholder register for BookSwap Campus v1 identifies 15 stakeholders across all four Mendelow quadrants using the `brainstorming-role-playing` strategy — team members assumed the roles of student buyer, seller, university authority, library staff, technical team, regulator, payment provider, and organized student community to avoid omissions. Each entry specifies interest (1–5), influence (1–5), attitude (Advocate / Neutral), channel, cadence, and relationship owner, with the sponsor (Dra. Mariana Peralta, Influence 5 / Interest 5) and the libraries' spokesperson (Lic. Paola Quintero, Influence 4 / Interest 5) in Manage Closely. Four open questions are tracked for resolution — including the payment provider's attitude and the formal mapping of IT and Finance contacts at La Salle and Panamericana — with named owners and deadlines before M1. [See full stakeholder register](/pm-kit/outputs/stakeholder-register/bookswap-campus-v1.md)

### Product Brief

The product brief documents the business case that led to the charter, grounding the problem in two figures from Servicios Escolares: students at the three pilot universities pay an average MXN 4,800 per semester on required textbooks, and 38% of first-year students reported skipping at least one required book for financial reasons. The brief introduces Sofía (first-semester buyer) and Diego (sixth-semester seller) as personas showing both sides of the unmet need, and proposes four functional building blocks — verified registration, ISBN catalog, held-in-escrow payment, and bilateral rating — together with a pre-launch campaign targeting 200 books published and 300 pre-registered users before go-live. Six key risks are documented with ownership and mitigation, including the critical-mass adoption risk and onboarding friction identified later by the Five Whys analysis. [See full product brief](/pm-kit/outputs/product-brief/bookswap-campus-v1.md)

## Planning

### Product Requirements Document

The BookSwap Campus v1 PRD was produced with the `prd` skill from the charter and stakeholder register, and defines 17 functional requirements (FR-001 to FR-017) organized around four user types: buyer student, seller student, university administrator, and platform moderator. Non-functional requirements mandate LFPDPPP data-privacy compliance, WCAG 2.1 AA accessibility, and a p95 response time under 2 seconds — figures the team set before writing a single line of code. Five open questions are tracked with named owners and resolution deadlines, including the legal status of escrow under Mexican payment regulation. [See full PRD](/pm-kit/outputs/prd/bookswap-campus-v1.md)

### Work Breakdown Structure

The BookSwap Campus v1 WBS was produced with the `wbs` skill and structures the project into 7 phases at Level 1 (Foundation through Closure), roughly 32 work packages at Level 3, and a Mermaid `graph TD` diagram that maps phases to sub-deliverables. Each package carries a WBS code, owner, estimated duration, and dependencies; the WBS dictionary calls out the five most ambiguous packages — including the escrow integration and the institutional email verification — whose acceptance criteria were unclear in the PRD. The WBS became the direct input for the schedule and for sprint backlog decomposition. [See full WBS](/pm-kit/outputs/wbs/bookswap-campus-v1.md)

### Epics and Stories

The BookSwap Campus v1 epics-and-stories artifact was produced with the `epics-and-stories` skill and decomposes the PRD into 7 epics (EP-01 through EP-07) and 18 user stories (ST-01 through ST-18). Priority distribution is 12 Must / 5 Should / 1 Could; every story includes Gherkin acceptance criteria (Given / When / Then) and explicit dependencies on prior stories, so the team entered sprint planning with a backlog that was already sequenced. EP-03 (Verified Onboarding) and EP-04 (Marketplace Core) carry the highest story count and map directly to the red-zone risks R1 and R2 in the risk matrix. [See full epics and stories](/pm-kit/outputs/epics-and-stories/bookswap-campus-v1.md)

### Schedule (Gantt)

The BookSwap Campus v1 schedule was produced with the `schedule-gantt` skill and spans approximately 160 business days on the critical path, from kick-off on 2026-05-04 (M0) to formal close on 2026-12-18 (M6), with go-live at M4 = 2026-08-12. The Mermaid `gantt` block defines 7 milestones and identifies parallel branches — UX design and infrastructure setup run concurrently with sprint development to preserve float — and the narrative calls out the five tasks with zero float that would delay go-live if they slip. The schedule is cross-referenced to the WBS so any slippage in a work package cascades visibly to its milestone. [See full schedule](/pm-kit/outputs/schedule-gantt/bookswap-campus-v1.md)

### Cost Estimation — Sprint 4

The Sprint 4 cost estimation was facilitated with the `cost-estimation` skill and combines two techniques: Planning Poker (Fibonacci) for the 10 user stories and PERT three-point estimation for the 2 infrastructure tasks. Planning Poker produced 68 total story points; against the team's observed velocity of ~40 pts/sprint from Sprint 3, the backlog requires ~1.7 sprints — so the team committed a 28-pt ceiling for Sprint 4 and planned to carry ~28 pts into Sprint 5 pending the payment-provider SDK assumption. The two PERT tasks produced a combined expected duration of 9.17 business days with an 80% confidence interval of [6.6 — 11.8 days]. [See full estimation](/pm-kit/outputs/cost-estimation/sprint-4-backlog.md)

### Risk Matrix

The baseline risk matrix (v1.0) was produced with the `risk-matrix` skill and identifies 12 risks across all four PMBOK categories (technical, external, organizational, project management). The initial snapshot places four risks in the red zone: R1 adoption below critical threshold and R2 onboarding friction (both scored 20), followed by R6 architecture under start-of-semester traffic and R8 listings containing copyrighted material (both scored 15). Every Top-5 risk carries a concrete response plan with actions, owner, trigger conditions, and target date (e.g., R2 is assigned to the Product Owner with a due date at end of Sprint 4, 2026-05-08). The artifact is cross-referenced with the charter: R2 guards the "median time-to-first-transaction ≤ 7 days" metric and R1 guards the 1,500-users end-of-semester objective. [See full matrix](/pm-kit/outputs/risk-matrix/bookswap-campus-v1-planning.md)

The plan documents who sends what to whom, at what cadence, and through which channel, covering everything from the daily standup to the quarterly steering-committee report for La Salle and Panamericana. A 2-year document retention policy and clear rules about when to escalate a blocker to the sponsor (unresolved after 2 business days at team level) are included. [See full communications plan](/pm-kit/outputs/communication-plan/bookswap-campus-v1.md)

### Quality Plan

The BookSwap Campus v1 quality plan was produced with the `quality-plan` skill and defines 9 measurable quality metrics with hard minimum targets, including p95 response time < 2 s, automated test coverage ≥ 70%, WCAG 2.1 AA compliance for all public screens, and OWASP Top 10 clearance confirmed by a penetration test before M3. Process quality is enforced via the SpecSafe five-step discipline — spec, tests, implementation, verify, archive — so that every story enters the sprint with acceptance criteria and exits with tests that pass. A penetration test and accessibility audit are scheduled as formal gates before go-live. [See full quality plan](/pm-kit/outputs/quality-plan/bookswap-campus-v1.md)

### Resource Plan

The BookSwap Campus v1 resource plan was produced with the `resource-plan` skill and assembles a core team of 5 (PM, PO, Eng Lead, backend developer, frontend/UX developer) plus 4 honorario student moderators for the pilot semester, with 4 TBD roles whose designation deadlines are tracked before M1. Total projected cost is MXN 1,357,000 — MXN 980,000 in personnel and MXN 377,000 in infrastructure, software licenses, and contingency — placing the project within the MXN 1.25M–1.75M envelope authorized in the charter. Open hiring tasks (two university liaisons at La Salle and Panamericana) are flagged with a hard deadline to avoid slipping the go-live milestone. [See full resource plan](/pm-kit/outputs/resource-plan/bookswap-campus-v1.md)

## What this demonstrates

BookSwap Campus was chosen as the worked example precisely because it is a real project with real stakes: a Mexican university initiative with a budget envelope, an academic calendar with hard deadlines, a complex stakeholder map spanning three institutions, and technical risks (escrow regulation, critical-mass adoption) that a cursory planning process would miss. Every artifact on this page was produced by the kit's skills in the order the playbook prescribes — and every artifact surfaces constraints, open questions, and risk owners that the team would have had to discover the hard way in execution.

The through-line of this case is the playbook's three core claims made visible: the agent enhances rather than replaces the project manager's judgment (the cost-estimation skill facilitates, but the team produces the numbers); each skill loads its authoritative source before it acts (the sprint-planning skill reads the 2020 Scrum Guide; the post-mortem skill opens with a blameless contract); and every artifact ends at a binary verification gate — PASS or FAIL — so that nothing moves forward on the basis of "the agent said it's done."
